Unequal Burden: A Mapping Tool for Environmental Justice in Cancer Alley

Exploring the Impact of Environmental Pollution on Public Health in Louisiana

Background on Cancer Alley

Cancer Alley is a stretch of the Mississippi River in Louisiana, known for having one of the highest concentrations of industrial pollution in the United States. The region, which spans from Baton Rouge to New Orleans, is home to numerous petrochemical plants, refineries, and hazardous waste sites. These industries have disproportionately impacted nearby communities, often affecting Black communities that live in close proximity to these toxic facilities. As a result, the area has seen significantly higher rates of cancer and other health issues compared to the national average.

This map visualizes key factors contributing to environmental injustice in Cancer Alley. By mapping variables such as population demographics, poverty rates, cancer incidence rates, and the locations of toxic release inventory (TRI) sites, the tool aims to shed light on how these factors intersect in the region. The visualizations allow users to better understand the spatial distribution of pollution sources and the corresponding health risks faced by local communities.

Through the "Unequal Burden" project, we aim to raise awareness and foster conversations about the ongoing challenges in Cancer Alley, hoping to inspire positive change for these vulnerable communities.
